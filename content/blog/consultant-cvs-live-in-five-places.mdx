Ask a People Ops or bid manager where the "real" version of a consultant's CV lives, and the honest answer is usually: it depends who you ask.

There's a copy on the shared drive, probably a few months old. There's whatever the consultant last emailed someone, which might be newer but isn't in the shared drive. There's a version an account manager reformatted for a specific client and never sent back upstream. And there's whatever's in the HRIS, which was accurate on the day it was imported and has drifted since.

None of these are wrong, exactly. They're just not the same document, and nobody owns reconciling them.

## Why this happens by default

CVs are treated as documents, not data. A Word file doesn't know it has five other versions floating around; it just sits wherever it was last saved. Every time someone needs a CV for a bid, they either:

- Track down what they believe is the latest version, or
- Ask the consultant to send an update, and wait.

Both are slow. Neither scales past a handful of bids a month.

## The cost is mostly invisible

This rarely shows up as a single dramatic failure. It shows up as:

- A bid manager spending an afternoon chasing five people for CV updates before a deadline.
- A client-facing CV that references a project the consultant hasn't worked on in two years, because nobody caught it.
- A new hire's CV not existing in any usable format for their first three months, because onboarding and CV creation are two separate, disconnected processes.

Add it up across a quarter and it's a meaningful chunk of a bid or delivery manager's time -- just not the kind that shows up on anyone's dashboard.

## Fixing it means changing what a CV *is*

The fix isn't a better shared drive. It's treating each consultant's experience as **structured data** -- skills, projects, certifications, tagged and searchable -- with CV documents generated *from* that data on demand, rather than being the data themselves.
