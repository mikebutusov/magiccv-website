"AI-native" gets attached to a lot of products lately, often loosely enough that it stops meaning much. For CV and proposal tools specifically, there's a concrete distinction worth drawing: **what does the tool start from?**

## Two starting points, two different tools

**Template-first, AI-assisted:** the tool starts from a CV template. You fill in or import content, and AI helps with phrasing, formatting, or suggestions along the way. The brief -- the actual RFP, the client's stated requirements -- comes in after the template is already the frame.

**AI-native, brief-first:** the tool starts from the RFP itself. It reads the win conditions and requirements first, and only then reasons about which real experience, from which consultants, actually answers them. The CV is an output of that reasoning, not the starting point.

The difference sounds subtle. In practice, it determines whether tailoring a CV to a brief is a manual, per-bid task, or something the tool does as a first step.

## Why the starting point matters

Template-first tools are good at formatting consistency. They're not built to answer the question that actually determines whether a CV is a strong fit for a brief: *does this person's real experience match what the client is asking for, and is that the experience being foregrounded?*

Answering that question well requires reading the brief closely -- the evaluation criteria, the specific skills called out, the shape of the engagement -- and then matching it against real, verifiable experience. That's a reasoning step, not a formatting step, and it's the part that's slow to do by hand for every bid.

## What this should never mean: fabrication

Worth stating plainly, because it's the obvious risk with "AI reasons about your CV": none of this should involve inventing experience. The only defensible version of AI-native CV generation is one where every claim in the output is traceable back to a real fact in the consultant's source profile -- re-emphasized and reordered to match the brief, never fabricated.

That's the specific design constraint behind [RFP-to-CV reasoning](/features/rfp-to-cv): read the brief first, reason about fit, and keep every generated claim traceable to source. There's also a conservative mode for teams who want a person to review suggested emphasis before anything is finalized, rather than trusting the output outright.

If you're evaluating tools in this category, "AI-native" is worth asking about directly: does the product start from the brief, or from a template with AI layered on top? The answer tends to predict a lot about how much manual tailoring work is actually removed.
