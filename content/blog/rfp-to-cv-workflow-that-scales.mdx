Most consulting firms don't have a CV problem when they're small. Five consultants, a handful of live RFPs a month, one person who knows where every CV lives -- it works, more or less, because one person is holding the whole thing together in their head.

The problem shows up on the way from ten RFPs a month to thirty. That's when the informal system -- a shared drive, a few Word templates, someone's memory of who's free and what they've worked on -- stops being informal-but-functional and starts being the reason bids go out late, or don't go out at all.

## The pattern that breaks first

Almost every firm we've talked to describes the same failure mode, in the same order:

1. **CVs drift out of date.** A consultant finishes a project, and their CV doesn't get updated for months -- if ever.
2. **Formatting eats the afternoon before a deadline.** Someone spends hours making five different CVs look like they came from the same firm.
3. **The best-fit person isn't obvious.** Without a searchable view of who's done what, staffing a bid becomes a Slack message asking "does anyone remember who worked on the retail migration project?"
4. **Quality becomes a function of who's free, not who's best.** Whoever has time to reformat a CV before the deadline gets used, not necessarily the strongest match.

None of these are dramatic on their own. Together, they add up to bids that go out later, look less consistent, and represent the team less accurately than they should.

## What "scales" actually means here

A CV workflow that scales isn't about more automation for its own sake. It's about removing the parts of the process that get worse as volume increases, while keeping the parts that require actual judgment.

Concretely, that means:

- **One current profile per consultant**, not a CV file that's someone's best guess as to the latest version.
- **Tailoring driven by the brief**, not a blank-page rewrite for every bid.
- **Consistent formatting by default**, not a manual pass before every submission.
- **A searchable view of skills and experience**, so staffing a bid doesn't depend on institutional memory.

The judgment that should stay human: which relationships matter for this client, which angle to take in the cover narrative, and the final read-through before anything goes out the door.
